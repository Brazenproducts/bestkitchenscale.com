Why You Need a Food Scale on Keto

Keto isn't forgiving with approximate measurements. When your daily carb target is 20–50g, a handful of almonds you think is "about an ounce" can be the difference between ketosis and a stall. The same goes for fat and protein — both of which have to hit specific targets for keto to work as a weight loss or therapeutic protocol.

Here's what eyeballing gets wrong:

  • Almonds: One handful can range from 20g to 50g depending on the person — a 2.5x difference in calories, fat, and carbs.
  • Cheese: What looks like "1 oz" of cheddar on a plate is usually 40–50g. Actual 1 oz = 28g.
  • Avocado: The difference between half and one avocado seems obvious, but a large Hass half can weigh as much as a full medium Hass.
  • Meat: Raw vs. cooked weight differs dramatically. 150g of raw chicken breast becomes ~110g cooked. If you're tracking cooked weight from a raw-weight database entry, you're way off.

A food scale solves every one of these problems. Weigh raw, log raw. Or weigh cooked, find the cooked entry. Either way, your macros are accurate — and accurate macros are how keto actually works.

💡 Keto tip: Always weigh ingredients raw when possible and use the raw-weight nutritional entry in your tracking app. Cooking changes weight through water loss, but the macros stay the same by weight.

What to Weigh on a Keto Diet

Not everything needs to be weighed every time. Here's a practical guide to what keto dieters typically weigh and when:

Always weigh:

  • Nuts and seeds — High fat/calorie density, very easy to overeat by eye
  • Cheese — A typical "slice" of cheddar is often 2–3x a proper serving
  • Nut butters — Tablespoon measurements are inconsistent; weigh in grams
  • Oils and butter — One tablespoon can vary enormously by how it's measured
  • Avocado — Size varies wildly; weight is the only consistent measure
  • Protein portions (meat, fish, eggs) — Critical for hitting protein targets without exceeding them

Useful to weigh (but not always necessary):

  • Vegetables — Most keto-friendly veggies are low enough in carbs that rough volumes work fine, except for borderline foods like onions or carrots
  • Berries — High in natural sugar; weigh if you're in strict ketosis
  • Dark chocolate — Easy to overdo

Generally skip the scale for:

  • Leafy greens (spinach, lettuce, arugula) — Very low carbs, negligible impact
  • Water, coffee, tea, sparkling water
  • Herbs and spices in normal cooking quantities

Keto Food Scale FAQ

Do I need to weigh food every meal on keto?

When you first start keto, yes — it's how you learn what correct portions actually look like. After a few months, experienced keto dieters can eyeball most portions within acceptable accuracy. But accurate tracking is what prevents stalls, so especially during the first 60 days, weigh consistently.

Should I weigh food raw or cooked on keto?

Raw is generally more accurate because cooked weights vary with cooking method. Grilled chicken loses more moisture than baked chicken, for example. Weigh raw and use the raw nutritional entry in your tracking app. If you must track cooked weight, find an entry specifically labeled "cooked" or "grilled" to match.

What accuracy do I need in a keto food scale?

1g increments are sufficient for all standard keto food tracking. You don't need a 0.1g scale unless you're also tracking supplements or medications by weight. All five scales on our list measure to 1g.

Will a food scale help me lose weight on keto?

A scale doesn't cause weight loss, but accurate tracking does remove one of the biggest variables that causes keto to stall. Most people who "aren't losing on keto" are underestimating their calories or carbs. A food scale removes that uncertainty.
