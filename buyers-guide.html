1. Capacity: How Much Can It Weigh?

Most kitchen scales have an 11 lb (5 kg) maximum capacity. For the vast majority of home cooks — making bread dough, portioning meat, weighing ingredients for a cake — 5 kg is more than enough.

Where you might need more capacity:

  • Home bread baking: A large batch of dough can run 3–4 kg. Still fine with a 5 kg scale, but you have very little headroom if you're also weighing the bowl.
  • Beer brewing: Grain bills for 5-gallon batches often exceed 5 kg. The My Weigh KD8000 (8 kg / 17.6 lb) was practically made for this.
  • Coffee roasting or bulk prep: Same story — bigger scales for bigger batches.

For keto tracking, meal prep, and everyday cooking, 11 lb / 5 kg is all you need. Don't pay extra for capacity you won't use.

Quick rule: If you're cooking for 1–4 people, a 5 kg scale is perfect. If you're feeding a large family or baking in bulk, consider 8 kg+.

2. Accuracy & Increments: How Precise Does It Need to Be?

Kitchen scales measure in increments — meaning each "step" the display jumps by. The most common increments are:

  • 1g increments — Standard for most digital scales. Fine for baking, cooking, and even most macro tracking.
  • 0.1g increments — Needed for espresso, precision spice blending, or pharmaceutical use. Some scales offer 0.1g in a lower-weight precision mode.
  • 2g or 5g increments — Found on cheaper or older scales. Not suitable for baking or dietary tracking.

All six scales on our list measure in 1g increments. The Escali Primo also measures to 0.1oz in imperial mode, which is useful for US-based recipes specifying partial ounces.

Accuracy vs. precision: Accuracy means the displayed weight matches the real weight. Precision means the scale gives the same reading consistently. Budget scales are often both less accurate and less precise — especially at weights under 5g. If you're weighing small amounts of spices, leavening agents, or coffee, this matters.

3. Tare Function: The Most Important Feature You Probably Take for Granted

The tare function (or "zero" button) resets the scale to zero with a container already on it. This means you can:

  • Place your mixing bowl on the scale, tare to zero, then weigh your flour directly into it.
  • Add flour, tare, add sugar, tare, add butter — all into the same bowl, tracking each ingredient separately.
  • Weigh items in bags or containers without counting the container's weight.

Every scale on our list has a tare function. This should be a non-negotiable requirement. If you're looking at a scale without tare, skip it.

Auto-tare: Some premium scales auto-tare when they detect a container is placed on the platform. Nice to have, but not a dealbreaker — a manual tare button works just as well.

4. Units & Measurement Modes

Most digital kitchen scales measure in at least grams (g) and ounces (oz). Better scales also include:

  • Pounds and ounces (lb:oz) — For US recipes specifying combined pound/ounce measurements.
  • Fluid ounces (fl oz) or milliliters (mL) — For liquids. Handy but not universal — calculated from water density, so not perfectly accurate for oils, milk, etc.
  • Grams (g) — The most useful mode. International standard. Required for virtually all serious baking recipes and nutritional tracking.

Our recommendation: make sure any scale you buy can display grams. Ounces are useful for US-format recipes, but grams are the precision standard for baking and macro tracking worldwide.

5. Platform Size: Does Your Bowl Fit?

This is a frequently overlooked spec. If you bake with large stand mixer bowls or stock pots, a tiny platform creates a problem: the bowl hangs over the edges, which doesn't affect accuracy — but it does affect whether you can read the display.

The OXO Good Grips solves this brilliantly with its pull-out display that extends beyond the platform edge — so even a large bowl placed dead-center won't block the readout. It's a design detail that matters a lot in practice.

General platform size guidance:

  • 6" x 6" or larger: Suitable for most bowls. The Etekcity, Escali, and Amazon Basics all fall in this range.
  • Large/extra-large platforms: The My Weigh KD8000 has a notably wide platform ideal for commercial-sized baking.

6. Digital vs. Analog Kitchen Scales

Analog (mechanical) kitchen scales use a spring mechanism with a dial display. They require no batteries and never need to be zeroed electronically. But they're less accurate, harder to read precisely, and don't have a tare function.

Digital scales win for almost every use case:

  • Higher accuracy (1g vs. typically 5–10g on analog)
  • Tare function
  • Multiple unit modes
  • Auto-off to save battery
  • Flatter profile — easier to store

The only reason to choose analog: you want zero maintenance and never want to deal with batteries. For everything else — baking, keto, meal prep, or coffee — digital is the clear choice.
