How to Use a Kitchen Scale for Baking and Cooking

Last updated: May 2026 · 5 min read

A kitchen scale is the single most useful tool for accurate baking and precise cooking — more useful than most gadgets people buy. If you've never used one (or just got one and aren't sure where to start), here's exactly how it works and how to get the most out of it.

Why Use a Kitchen Scale?

Volume measurements (cups, tablespoons) are inherently imprecise. A cup of flour can vary by 20–30% in weight depending on how densely it's packed. In baking, where the ratio of flour to liquid to leavening is critical, that difference determines whether your bread rises properly or your cookies come out flat. Weight measurements are objective — a gram is always a gram.

Beyond baking, scales are useful for portion control, following macros, cooking by weight (many professional recipes), and reducing dishes (weigh directly into mixing bowls).

Understanding Your Scale's Key Features

The Tare Function

The most important button on any kitchen scale. "Tare" resets the display to zero while accounting for the weight of whatever's currently on the scale. This lets you:

  • Place a bowl on the scale, press Tare, and have the scale read zero — then add your ingredient and see only its weight
  • Add multiple ingredients to the same bowl sequentially: add the first ingredient, note the weight, press Tare to zero out, add the second ingredient
  • Use any container without having to manually subtract its weight

The Tare button is the feature that makes kitchen scales practical for real cooking. Learn to use it automatically.

Unit Switching

Most scales can toggle between grams, ounces, and sometimes milliliters (for water) or pounds. Look for a "Unit" or "g/oz" button on your scale. Most baking recipes from outside the US use grams; American recipes often use ounces. Being able to switch between them means any recipe is usable.

Display and Resolution

Entry-level scales typically measure in 1-gram or 2-gram increments. Better scales (like the OXO Good Grips or Escali Primo) measure in 1-gram increments for higher accuracy. For most home baking, 1-gram resolution is sufficient. For brewing, coffee, or very small measurements (spices, yeast), you may want a scale accurate to 0.1g — called a jeweler's or precision scale.

Step-by-Step: How to Weigh Ingredients

Basic Method

  1. Place your scale on a flat, stable surface
  2. Turn it on and let it stabilize at zero
  3. Place your bowl or container on the scale
  4. Press Tare — the display returns to 0.0
  5. Add your ingredient until the display shows the desired weight
  6. Note the weight or move to the next step

Measuring Multiple Ingredients Into One Bowl

  1. Place the bowl on the scale, press Tare
  2. Add ingredient #1 until you reach target weight → note or proceed
  3. Press Tare to reset to zero (bowl + ingredient #1 weight is now baseline)
  4. Add ingredient #2 until you reach its target weight
  5. Press Tare again
  6. Repeat for each ingredient

This is the professional method — fewer bowls, faster workflow, no measuring cups to wash.

Reading a Recipe That Uses Weight

Modern baking recipes (especially from professional bakers and European sources) list ingredients by weight:

All-purpose flour: 240g
Sugar: 150g
Butter: 115g
Eggs: 100g (about 2 large)

Just work through each ingredient in sequence, taring between each one. The recipe becomes much easier to follow than the equivalent measuring cup version.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not pressing Tare after placing the container. You'll weigh the bowl's weight as part of your ingredient — the most common beginner mistake.
  • Adding too much and trying to take away. Add ingredients slowly, especially when approaching the target. It's easier to add more than to remove excess.
  • Pressing Tare with more than the max capacity on the scale. If your bowl is heavy and you've added several ingredients, check that you haven't exceeded the scale's max weight before taring again.
  • Placing the scale on an unstable surface. Scales measure vibration. A wobbly table will give fluctuating readings.
  • Letting liquids get under the scale platform. Most scales aren't waterproof. Liquid under the weighing platform can damage sensors. Put a towel nearby and be careful with liquids.

Cleaning and Maintenance

Wipe the weighing surface with a damp cloth after use. Don't submerge the scale or let liquid run into the seam between the platform and the body. For scales with removable platforms (like many OXO models), remove and wash the platform separately.

Choosing a Scale for Different Uses

  • General baking and cooking: Any 1g-resolution scale up to 5kg (11 lbs) capacity. The Escali Primo is reliable and inexpensive.
  • Coffee and tea: Look for 0.1g resolution and a fast response time for pour-over brewing.
  • Portioning large quantities (bread dough, meal prep): Higher capacity (5kg+) with at least 1g resolution.
  • Macro tracking: Standard 1g resolution is fine.

Once you start weighing ingredients instead of measuring by volume, going back feels like a step backward. The accuracy improvement is immediately noticeable, especially in baking — your results become more consistent and your recipes more repeatable.
