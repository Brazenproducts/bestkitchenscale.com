How to Calibrate a Kitchen Scale (and Know If Yours Is Off)

Last updated: May 2026 · 5 min read

Kitchen scales drift over time. A scale that was accurate when new may be reading 5–10g high or low a year later, and you'd never know unless you tested it. For most cooking, small errors don't matter. For baking, coffee, and dietary tracking, they do. Here's how to check your scale's accuracy and recalibrate it when needed.

Signs Your Scale May Be Off

Watch for these indicators that something is wrong:

  • Inconsistent readings: You weigh the same object three times and get three different numbers
  • Baking results getting worse: Recipes that used to work consistently now come out differently
  • The scale doesn't zero out correctly: You turn it on with nothing on it and it reads -1g or +2g
  • Readings change when you move the scale: Results differ depending on where on the counter the scale is
  • It's been dropped or knocked off the counter: Impact can shift the calibration

The Easiest Accuracy Test: Calibration Weights

The most reliable way to test accuracy is with calibration weights — small, precision-manufactured metal weights that are certified to a known mass. You can buy a basic set of calibration weights on Amazon for $8–$15.

  1. Turn on the scale and let it stabilize at zero on a flat, stable surface
  2. Place a known weight on the scale (e.g., a 100g calibration weight)
  3. Read the display. It should read exactly 100g
  4. If it reads 98g or 103g, your scale is off by that amount
  5. Test with multiple calibration weights to see if the error is consistent or variable

A consistent offset (always reads 3g low) is a calibration issue. Variable, unpredictable readings indicate a faulty sensor — the scale may need replacement.

The Coin Method (Quick Rough Check)

If you don't have calibration weights, a U.S. nickel weighs exactly 5.000 grams and a U.S. penny (post-1982) weighs exactly 2.500 grams. These are manufactured to tight tolerances and can serve as quick reference weights:

  • Place a nickel on the scale → should read 5.00g
  • Place four nickels → should read 20.00g
  • Stack ten nickels → should read 50.00g

This method is less precise than certified calibration weights (coins wear and can vary slightly), but it's a useful quick check for obvious errors.

How to Calibrate Your Scale

The calibration process varies by scale model. There are three common approaches:

Method 1: Built-In Calibration Mode

Many scales (particularly mid-range and professional models) have a calibration function accessible through button combinations. Common procedures:

  1. With the scale turned off, hold the On/Tare button while turning it on, or
  2. Press and hold the Unit button for several seconds while on
  3. The display typically shows "CAL" and then a target weight (e.g., "100g")
  4. Place that exact weight on the scale when prompted
  5. The scale re-calibrates itself and returns to normal operation

Check your scale's manual for the specific procedure — it varies by brand. Escali, OXO, Ozeri, and Etekcity all have different calibration sequences, and some models don't support user calibration at all.

Method 2: Span Calibration (Zero + Full Range)

Some advanced scales calibrate at two points: empty (zero) and at full capacity or a specified test weight. This corrects for non-linear drift across the measurement range, giving better accuracy at all weight levels.

Method 3: The Scale Can't Be User-Calibrated

Many entry-level scales have fixed calibration set at the factory and no user-accessible calibration mode. If yours falls into this category and is reading inaccurately, your options are:

  • Contact the manufacturer — some will recalibrate or replace out-of-spec scales under warranty
  • Use a known offset: if your scale consistently reads 3g high, mentally subtract 3g from every reading until you replace the scale
  • Replace the scale — budget options under $15 aren't worth the hassle of working around poor accuracy

Environmental Factors That Affect Accuracy

Even a well-calibrated scale can give bad readings in certain conditions:

  • Unstable surface: Vibrations from nearby appliances (dishwasher, blender) or an uneven counter affect readings. Use a flat, stable surface.
  • Air currents: Fans, open windows, or HVAC vents cause readings to fluctuate. This matters most when weighing very small amounts (under 5g).
  • Temperature extremes: Most scales specify an operating temperature range (typically 50–95°F). A scale in a cold garage or very hot kitchen may read inaccurately.
  • Magnetic fields: Rare in home kitchens but relevant — don't place the scale directly on a magnetic induction cooktop.
  • Low battery: Many scales give erratic or consistently incorrect readings as the battery dies. Replace batteries if readings become unreliable.

How Often to Calibrate

For home use:

  • Test accuracy when you notice inconsistent results
  • After the scale has been dropped or knocked around
  • Every 6–12 months as preventive maintenance if you use it daily for precision applications (coffee, baking, dietary tracking)
  • After replacing the batteries (battery changes can sometimes shift calibration on certain models)

A calibrated scale is a reliable scale. For the cost of a $10 calibration weight set, you can verify accuracy anytime and trust your measurements completely — which is the whole point of using a scale in the first place.
