How to Measure Food for Keto (and Why a Scale Beats Measuring Cups)

Last updated: May 2026 · 5 min read

Keto works because of careful macronutrient ratios: typically 70–75% fat, 20–25% protein, and 5–10% carbohydrates. Staying in ketosis requires keeping net carbs low — usually under 20–50g per day depending on the individual. To hit those numbers accurately, you need to measure your food. And measuring cups just aren't precise enough.

Why Measuring Cups Fail for Keto Tracking

The fundamental problem with cups is that they measure volume, not mass. A cup of shredded cheddar can weigh anywhere from 80g to 130g depending on how loosely or tightly it's packed. That's a 60% weight difference — and the nutrition facts on the package are listed per gram, not per cup.

On keto, where even an extra 10–15g of carbs can push someone out of ketosis, this kind of imprecision compounds over the course of a day. You think you're at 18g net carbs; you're actually at 28g. That's the difference between staying in ketosis and not.

A kitchen scale eliminates this variable. 100g of shredded cheese is always 100g.

How to Weigh Food for Keto: Step-by-Step

  1. Open your tracking app (MyFitnessPal, Cronometer, Carb Manager — Cronometer is generally most accurate for micronutrients).
  2. Search for your food item in the app. Find the entry that lists the serving size in grams (not cups or tablespoons).
  3. Place your plate or bowl on the scale and press Tare to zero it out.
  4. Add your food until you reach your desired portion in grams.
  5. Log that gram weight in your tracking app.
  6. Tare the scale again and add your next food item.

The whole process takes 30 seconds per ingredient once you're practiced. Prepping a whole meal is just 2–3 minutes of actual weighing time.

Key Foods to Weigh on Keto

Not everything needs to be weighed obsessively — but certain foods have enough carb density that precision matters:

  • Nuts and nut butters: Easy to over-eat. 1 oz of almonds (28g) = 6g carbs. A "handful" could be 40–60g easily.
  • Berries: Allowed on keto in small amounts, but portion-dependent. 100g of blueberries = ~12g net carbs. 200g doubles that.
  • Cheese: Low in carbs but calorie-dense. Consistent portioning keeps total intake in check.
  • Avocado: Healthy fat but variable size. Weigh to know exactly how much you're eating.
  • Vegetables: Most low-carb vegetables are fine in generous amounts, but starchy ones (carrots, onions, bell peppers) accumulate. Weigh to know.
  • Protein: Raw weight vs. cooked weight differs significantly. Weigh raw for most accurate tracking, or use cooked weight entries in your app.

Raw vs. Cooked Weight: A Critical Distinction

Meat loses 20–30% of its weight during cooking due to moisture and fat loss. 200g of raw chicken breast becomes approximately 140–150g cooked. This matters because most tracking apps have both raw and cooked entries — make sure you're logging accordingly.

  • Best practice: Weigh protein raw before cooking, log raw weight with the "raw" entry in your app
  • Alternative: Weigh cooked and use the "cooked" entry — just don't mix them
  • Never: Use a raw weight but log a cooked entry (or vice versa) — you'll under- or overestimate protein significantly

Tracking Fat on Keto

Fat is the primary fuel source on keto, but it's also the most calorie-dense macronutrient (9 calories per gram vs 4 for protein/carbs). Weighing fat sources keeps your total caloric intake in check while ensuring you're hitting your fat macros:

  • 1 tablespoon of olive oil = ~14g = 120 calories. Most people pour more than a tablespoon without realizing it.
  • A slice of bacon varies widely in fat content by thickness and brand — weighing is more accurate than "1 slice"
  • Butter: measure by weight for precise macro tracking; the tablespoon conversion is approximate

  • Gram + ounce switching: Useful since some apps display one and some the other

Practical Tips for Daily Keto Weighing

  • Keep the scale on the counter, not in a cabinet. Out-of-sight means out-of-use. Accessibility drives consistency.
  • Weigh when possible, estimate when necessary. A meal at home is easy to weigh. A meal at a restaurant isn't — make your best estimate and move on. Don't let perfectionism become an excuse not to track.
  • Batch weigh and pre-portion. If you meal prep, weigh everything once during prep and divide into containers. Then tracking each meal is just logging one pre-weighed container.
  • Double-check database entries. Food databases in apps have user-submitted errors. If a food's macros look wrong, cross-reference with the USDA FoodData Central database.

Accurate measurement is what makes keto work long-term. The scale takes the guesswork out of it — and once it becomes habit, it adds almost no friction to meal prep.
