Grams vs. Ounces in Baking: Why Grams Are More Accurate

Last updated: May 2026 · 5 min read

If you've ever browsed a professional baking recipe or followed a recipe from a UK, European, or Australian source, you've seen ingredients listed in grams. American recipes tend to use cups and ounces. Is there actually a practical difference? Yes — and understanding it will improve your baking.

The Fundamental Difference

Grams are a metric unit of mass. Ounces in a baking context are typically weight ounces (avoirdupois ounces) — also mass. So technically both measure weight. The difference isn't philosophical — it's practical.

Grams are smaller units. One ounce equals 28.35 grams. This means:

  • A recipe calling for 2 oz of an ingredient is asking for ~56.7 grams
  • A small error of 0.5 oz is a 14-gram error — potentially significant in baking
  • In grams, you can measure 114 grams vs 115 grams. In ounces, 4 oz vs 4.1 oz is a resolution that many scales don't accurately display

Essentially: grams provide finer resolution by default. A 1-gram scale reading in grams is more precise than a scale reading in 0.1 oz increments, because 0.1 oz = 2.83 grams.

Why Professional Bakers Default to Grams

Commercial bakeries, culinary school curricula, and most serious baking books use grams for several reasons:

  • Scalability: Grams scale linearly and easily. If you want to make 1.5x a recipe, multiply every gram measurement by 1.5. No awkward fractional cups or confusing unit conversions.
  • Precision at small quantities: When a recipe calls for 3 grams of salt or 2 grams of yeast, using ounces (0.1 oz and 0.07 oz respectively) is difficult to read accurately on most scales.
  • International standard: The metric system is universal. A French pâtisserie recipe and a Japanese milk bread recipe both use grams — consistent, comparable, interoperable.
  • Easier to verify: When scaling a commercial recipe from 100 loaves to 50 loaves, working in grams (dividing all numbers by 2) is simpler than dealing with mixed cup/ounce fractions.

The Volume Measurement Problem

American recipes often mix weight ounces AND volume measurements (cups), which creates extra confusion. "8 ounces of flour" means 8 weight ounces — but if you mistakenly interpret it as a volume cup, you're off by a significant margin.

Grams are always weight/mass. There's no ambiguity. This is one reason professional recipes are written in grams: the units are unambiguous.

Does It Actually Matter for Home Baking?

For some things, not much. A stew, a soup, most savory cooking — the precision doesn't matter enough to affect results.

For baking, especially:

  • Bread and yeast-risen doughs
  • Macarons, soufflés, and technically precise pastry
  • Recipes with small quantities of leavening agents (baking powder, baking soda, yeast)
  • Any recipe where you've had inconsistent results before

...switching to grams makes a noticeable difference. The extra precision translates directly to more consistent results.

Quick Conversion Reference

  • 1 oz = 28.35g
  • 1 lb = 453.6g
  • 1 cup all-purpose flour ≈ 120–130g (depending on packing)
  • 1 cup granulated sugar = 200g
  • 1 cup butter = 227g (2 sticks)
  • 1 tablespoon = ~14g for most solid ingredients
  • 1 teaspoon = ~4–5g for most ingredients

Note: volume-to-gram conversions vary by ingredient. This is the whole point — a cup of flour and a cup of sugar weigh different amounts. Grams tell you exactly what you have; cups tell you the volume but not the mass.

Setting Your Scale to Grams

If your scale defaults to ounces or pounds, find the Unit button (sometimes labeled "g/oz" or just "Unit") and toggle until it reads "g." Most modern kitchen scales — including popular options like the OXO Good Grips and Escali Primo — switch units easily. Some display a mode indicator light that shows which unit is active.

If you're working from American recipes that use cups and tablespoons, a simple trick: find a gram-based version of the same recipe type online (there are excellent weight-based American baking resources), or use a reliable cup-to-gram conversion chart and convert once, writing the gram amounts next to the recipe.

The Bottom Line

Grams are more accurate for baking because they're a smaller unit, they have no ambiguity, and they scale easily. If you're going to use a kitchen scale (which you should), learn to think in grams. Your baked goods will be more consistent, your recipes more repeatable, and troubleshooting failures much easier.
