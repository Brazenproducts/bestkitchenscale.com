Boulder Tools vs ARB Speedy Seal Series II

Two of the best tire repair kits on Amazon — head to head. Which one is actually right for you?

The short answer

If you're picking one for the trunk of your daily driver, get the Boulder Tools Heavy Duty Kit. If you're packing for a trail rig, overland build, or expedition, get the ARB Speedy Seal Series II. Both kits will plug your tire — the difference is everything around the plug.

Tools and plugs

Both kits use forged-steel reamer and insertion tools with rubber-overmolded grips, and both come with the brown self-vulcanizing string plugs that are the professional standard. Side by side, the actual reamer and insertion tools are functionally equivalent — we couldn't tell the difference once they were in our hands. Both have held up to dozens of plug installations during testing without forming burrs or losing grip.

ARB ships 40 plugs versus Boulder Tools' 30, which is a small win for the ARB if you're going to use the kit a lot. For most drivers, you'll never use more than 5 plugs in the lifetime of the kit, so this difference is mostly academic.

The case is the real difference

The Boulder Tools kit comes in a fitted hard-plastic case roughly the size of a large hardcover book — perfect for a glovebox, a seat-back pocket, or a tool drawer. It's not an outdoor case; it's an organizer.

The ARB Speedy Seal Series II case is a different animal. It's overbuilt — secure latches, denser plastic, designed to live in a roof rack or behind a 4Runner's third row and bounce around without popping open. If you're equipping a daily driver, that's overkill weight and bulk. If you've ever had a Pelican-style case knocked open in a UTV, you know why ARB built it this way.

What's in the box

This is where ARB earns its $20–$35 premium. Both kits include reamer, insertion tool, plugs, knife, and basic accessories. ARB adds:

  • A pair of work gloves (you will get rubber cement on your hands without them)
  • A tire pressure gauge (so you actually know what pressure you're inflating to)
  • A full set of valve cores, valve caps, and a valve core remover
  • Soapy-water bottle for finding leaks
  • Cleaning rag and brush

Each of those things you can buy separately for a few bucks. Together, in one organized case, they're the difference between a 5-minute trail repair and a frustrating roadside scavenger hunt.

Which should you buy?

Buy the Boulder Tools kit if: You drive on pavement 99% of the time, you want the cheaper of the two, and a glovebox-sized kit is what you have room for. Check Boulder Tools price
